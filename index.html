How Does It Work?

Our GPA Calculator accuracy depends on two things: the accuracy of your input and the use of the official Law School Admission Council grading scale methodology.

Our calculation logic mirrors the process used by LSAC's Credential Assembly Service (CAS), ensuring that it closely aligns with the way law schools evaluate academic records.

Below is a clear, step-by-step explanation of how our tool calculates your GPA.

1. Getting User Data:

This grade calculator relies on the information you provide, including course grades and credit hours. These inputs form the basis of the grade point calculation and directly impact the final result.

To ensure accuracy, you must enter grades and credit hours exactly as they appear on your official academic transcripts. Even smaller discrepancies can affect the estimated GPA. So, make sure to put in correct information.

2. Converting Your Grades:

After entering, each grade is converted to a standard numerical value, based on the official LSAC Grade Conversion Table. This is the same grading scale used by LSAC when they calculate CAS GPAs.

This conversion step is important because LSAC grade values can differ from the institutional GPA scale. This step makes our tool accurate. Applying the correct LSAC values ensures consistency with how law schools evaluate applications.

3. Applying the GPA Formula:

After converting the grades, this tool calculates total quality points by multiplying each grade value by its corresponding credit hours. Then a standard GPA formula is used. which is

GPA = Σ (Credit Hours * Letter Grade Value) / Total Credit Hours

This is the same weighted GPA formula used across standardized academic evaluations, including LSAC's CAS GPA calculation.

How Does LSAC Calculate GPA?

The Law School Admission Council (LSAC) does not use your university GPA exactly as it appears on your transcript. Instead, LSAC recalculates your undergraduate GPA through the Credential Assembly Service (CAS) to ensure a fair and consistent evaluation for all applicants.

Now let's see how LSAC calculates GPA.

Transcript Review:

LSAC collects transcripts from each undergraduate institution you attended. This includes all courses completed before your first bachelor’s degree. That ensures a complete, accurate record of your academic history. After that, they review them.

Grade Standardization:

Since universities use different grading systems, LSAC converts each grade into a numerical value using its official LSAC Grade Conversion Table. This standardizes all grades to a common 4.33 GPA scale, allowing comparison across schools and education systems.

Weighted GPA Calculation:

Each converted grade is multiplied by its corresponding credit hours to calculate quality points. Courses with higher credit hours have a greater impact on your final GPA.

This weighted approach ensures that your GPA accurately reflects the importance of each course.

Final CAS GPA:

After processing all coursework, LSAC calculates the final CAS GPA by adding all grade points together and dividing the total by the number of credit hours, which is reported to law schools. This standardized GPA is used for:

  • Admissions decisions
  • Scholarship evaluations
  • Comparing applicants from different universities and grading systems

This standardized method allows law schools to fairly compare applicants from different universities and education systems. Your LSAC GPA may differ from your university GPA due to variations in grading scales and conversion rules. This ensures fairness, transparency, and consistency for all applicants.

LSAC GPA vs. University GPA

Your university GPA is calculated by your institution using its own grading scale and academic policies, which can vary widely between colleges and countries.

Your LSAC GPA, on the other hand, is recalculated by LSAC through the Credential Assembly Service (CAS) using a standardized 4.0 scale.

Because LSAC applies uniform conversion rules to all transcripts, your LSAC GPA may differ from your university GPA. Law schools rely on the LSAC CAS GPA, not your original university GPA, when evaluating applications.
